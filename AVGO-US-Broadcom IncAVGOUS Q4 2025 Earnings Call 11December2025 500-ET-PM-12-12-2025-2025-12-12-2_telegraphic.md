**Hock E. Tan — President, CEO & Director, Broadcom Inc.**

**FY2025 Full-Year Recap**
- Consolidated revenue grew 24% YoY to record $64B; driven by AI semiconductors and VMware
- AI revenue grew 65% YoY to $20B; drove semiconductor revenue to record $37B for year
- Infrastructure software revenue grew 26% YoY to $27B on strong VCF adoption
- AI spending momentum expected to accelerate further in 2026

**Q4 FY2025 Results**
- Total revenue record $18B, up 28% YoY; above guidance on better-than-expected AI semiconductor and infrastructure software growth
- Q4 consolidated adjusted EBITDA record $12.12B, up 34% YoY
- Semiconductor revenue $11.1B; YoY growth accelerated to 35%
- AI semiconductor revenue $6.5B, up 74% YoY; represents >10x growth trajectory over 11 quarters reported
- Custom accelerator (XPU) business more than doubled YoY
- XPUs used for training LLMs and monetizing via inferencing APIs/applications
- XPUs not only for internal workloads — extended externally; e.g., Google TPUs used for Gemini also deployed for Apple, Cohere, SSI for AI cloud computing; scale of this could be significant
- Q3 FY2025: received $10B order for latest TPU (Ironwood) racks to Anthropic — fourth customer
- Q4 FY2025: received additional $11B order from same customer (Anthropic) for delivery late 2026
- Other two existing customers prefer to control own destiny; continuing multiyear journey to own custom XPU racks
- Q4: acquired fifth XPU customer via $1B order for delivery late 2026
- AI networking demand stronger than XPUs — customers building data center infrastructure ahead of deploying accelerators
- Current order backlog for AI switches exceeds $10B
- Tomahawk 6 switch (102 Tb/s, first and only of its capability) booking at record rates
- Record orders secured on DSPs, optical components (lasers), PCIe switches for AI data centers
- Total AI order backlog (XPUs + networking components) exceeds $73B; ~half of Broadcom's consolidated backlog of $162B
- $73B AI backlog expected delivered over next 18 months
- Q1 FY2026 AI revenue expected to double YoY to $8.2B

**Non-AI Semiconductors**
- Q4 non-AI semiconductor revenue $4.6B, up 2% YoY, up 16% sequentially on wireless seasonality
- Broadband showed solid YoY recovery; wireless flat; all other end markets down; enterprise spending shows limited recovery signs
- Q1 non-AI semiconductor revenue forecast ~$4.1B, flat YoY, down sequentially due to wireless seasonality

**Infrastructure Software**
- Q4 infrastructure software revenue $6.9B, up 19% YoY; above outlook of $6.7B
- Q4 total contract value booked exceeded $10.4B vs. $8.2B a year ago
- Infrastructure software backlog ended year at $73B, up from $49B a year ago
- Q1 infrastructure software revenue forecast ~$6.8B; renewals expected seasonal
- FY2026 infrastructure software revenue still expected to grow low-double-digits %

**FY2026 Directional Outlook**
- AI revenue to continue accelerating; drive most of growth
- Non-AI semiconductor revenue to be stable
- Infrastructure software driven by VMware growth at low-double digits
- Q1 FY2026 consolidated revenue guidance ~$19.1B, up 28% YoY
- Q1 FY2026 adjusted EBITDA expected ~67% of revenue

**Q&A**

**Vivek Arya — BofA Securities**
- Asked: confirms $73B AI backlog over 18 months implies ~$50B+ for FY2026; also asked about customer-owned tooling risk and XPU content/share at largest customer

**Hock E. Tan**
- Confirms $73B AI backlog as of now; expects additional bookings over that period — $73B is minimum, not ceiling
- Bookings accelerating across XPUs, switches, DSPs, all AI data center components
- Tomahawk 6 (102 Tb/s) one of fastest-growing switch products ever shipped; only product of its kind
- Exact product needed to expand clusters of latest GPUs and XPUs
- Customer-owned tooling "overblown hypothesis" — does not expect it to materialize
- Custom XPU multiyear strategic journey; not transactional decision
- In hardware you can achieve far better performance vs. general-purpose GPU running on software/kernels
- TPU and other custom accelerators demonstrably better in Sparse Core, training, inference, reasoning
- LLM players must allocate resources carefully to compete, including against merchant GPU vendors not slowing evolution

**Ross Seymore — Deutsche Bank**
- Asked: are TPUs going to third-party customers substituting for ASICs with Broadcom, or broadening the market?

**Hock E. Tan**
- TPU adoption by third parties primarily substitutes merchant GPUs, not custom ASICs
- Moving GPU→TPU = transactional; investing in own custom accelerator = long-term strategic, multi-year commitment
- Strategic XPU investment not deterred by TPU-as-a-service model

**Harlan Sur — JPMorgan**
- Asked: confirms $73B backlog is snapshot; additional orders for quarters 4-6 still possible so backlog will grow; asks about 3nm/2nm wafer supply, CoWos, substrate, HBM commitments; asks about Singapore advanced packaging facility

**Hock E. Tan**
- Confirms $73B = minimum floor for next 18 months; more orders expected for shipments within same 6-quarter window
- Lead times range 6 months to 1 year depending on product
- Advanced packaging challenge growing as multi-chip designs proliferate in every custom accelerator
- Building substantial Singapore facility specifically for advanced packaging — insourcing for supply chain security and delivery, not just cost
- Silicon supply: reliant on TSMC for 2nm and 3nm; no constraint currently but will monitor as backlog builds

**CJ Muse — Cantor Fitzgerald**
- Asked: how does Broadcom content grow generation-to-generation for custom silicon; also, competitor announced CPX accelerator-for-accelerator for large context windows — could existing five customers need multiple XPU variants?

**Hock E. Tan**
- Each of five customers can create multiple XPU variants for training and inference — effectively two parallel tracks simultaneously per customer
- Custom accelerators enable unique hardware differentiation vs. software/kernel approach
- Examples: Sparse Core data routers vs. dense matrix multipliers on same chip; variation in memory capacity/bandwidth per chip
- Different hardware needed for different inference workloads: reasoning vs. decoding vs. prefill
- Multiple chips per customer already occurring; substantial variation underway

**Harsh Kumar — Piper Sandler**
- Asked: Q1 AI guide implies ~$1.7B sequential growth — how diverse is growth across three existing customers? Also: competitor acquisition of photonic fabric company — disruptive or gimmickry?

**Hock E. Tan**
- Sequential AI growth driven by mixed bag of existing customers on existing and new XPUs
- Networking demand also very strong: Tomahawk 5 + Tomahawk 6 switches, latest 1.6 Tb/s DSPs for optical scale-out interconnects, plus lasers and PIN diodes "going nuts"
- Of $73B AI backlog: ~$20B is everything except XPUs; remainder (~$53B) is XPUs
- $20B non-XPU still substantial
- Silicon photonics: sees it as necessary technology in the future but "not anytime soon"
- Broadcom has silicon photonic technology developed for 400G, progressing to 800G, now doing 1.6 Tb/s silicon photonic switches
- Engineers will continue to use copper for scale-up within rack as long as possible; pluggable optics for scale-out until that fails — only then silicon photonics becomes dominant
- Broadcom ready for silicon photonics when needed
